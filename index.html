Section 04
Ten Non-Obvious, High-Impact Findings
Evidence-grounded insights derived from statistical analysis of 2,496 hospital records
01
Financial Separation
Closures Exhibit Structural Insolvency, Not Cyclical Distress
Closed hospitals show a mean total margin of −6.7% versus +6.2% for open hospitals — a 12.9 percentage-point structural gap confirmed at p<0.001 (t=8.9). Patient services margin of −13.0% for closed facilities reveals clinical operations alone could not sustain viability. This is not cyclical — it is architectural.
Effect magnitude: 12.9pp total margin gap · t=8.902 · p<0.000001 · n=2,383
02
Program Failure
The MDH Designation Has Effectively Ceased to Exist
Medicare-Dependent Hospitals — designed for facilities with 60%+ Medicare volume — achieved a 94.4% closure rate. 17 of 18 MDH hospitals closed. The program, repeatedly extended via short-term legislation without structural reform, delayed closures without preventing them. Congress allowed MDH to expire after 2022 without a replacement framework.
MDH closure rate: 94.4% (17/18) · Avg patient margin: −21.3% · Avg expenses: $25.5M
03
Hidden Crisis
665 Open Hospitals Are on the Closure Trajectory
Using a conservative threshold — total margin below −5% or patient margin below −10% — 28.9% of currently open rural hospitals qualify as high-risk. Rural Emergency Hospitals (REH), the newest federal designation, show an 81.8% high-risk rate among open facilities, suggesting the REH conversion model is not financially stabilizing hospitals.
665 at-risk open hospitals · REH: 81.8% high-risk · CAH: 28.8% · IPPS: 30.1%
04
Policy Evidence
Medicaid Non-Expansion Doubles Rural Hospital Closure Risk
Non-expansion states exhibit an 11.6% rural hospital closure rate versus 5.8% in expansion states — a 2.0× differential representing the strongest single policy-attributable effect in the dataset. Non-expansion states (TX, TN, AL, FL, KS, OK, SC, MS) account for a disproportionate share of closures relative to their hospital counts.
Non-expansion: 11.6% · Expansion: 5.8% · Ratio: 2.0× · 101 vs 95 closures
05
Positive Signal
CAH Cost-Based Reimbursement Is the Most Effective Federal Protection
Critical Access Hospitals — receiving 101% of allowable Medicare costs — have a 4.9% closure rate, the lowest of any major designation, despite comprising 58% of rural hospitals. The CAH model's structural payment protection is necessary but not sufficient: 28.8% of open CAHs remain high-risk, indicating operational challenges the payment model cannot fully offset.
CAH closure rate: 4.9% · 1,452 total · 71 closed · 398 high-risk open (28.8%)
06
Geographic Pattern
The South Carries a Disproportionate Share of the Crisis
Tennessee (20.6%), Florida (26.7%), Alabama (16.4%), and North Carolina (17.6%) lead in closure rates among high-volume states. These states combine Medicaid non-expansion, high rural poverty, and smaller hospital sizes. Texas leads in absolute closures (25) due to volume. Six Southern states account for 83 closures — 42% of the national total from 15% of hospitals.
TX: 25 · TN: 14 · NC: 12 · AL: 10 · FL: 8 (26.7% rate) · OK: 10
07
Scale Paradox
IPPS Hospitals Close at 3.8× the CAH Rate Despite Higher Revenue
Inpatient Prospective Payment System hospitals — larger acute-care facilities averaging $91M in expenses — have an 18.8% closure rate versus 4.9% for CAHs averaging $43M. Scale alone does not confer resilience without the right payment architecture. IPPS rural hospitals face volume constraints identical to smaller facilities but lack cost-based reimbursement protection.
IPPS closure rate: 18.8% · CAH rate: 4.9% · Ratio: 3.8× · 88 of 467 IPPS closed
08
Best Practice
RRC and SCH Designations Demonstrate Consistently Stable Financials
Referral Regional Centers average +11.0% total margin with a 2.0% closure rate. Sole Community Hospitals average +4.7% margin with a 3.2% closure rate. Both carry enhanced Medicare payments and geographic protection. RCH (Rural Community Hospitals) show 0% closures. These programs offer a replicable structural model for stabilization.
RRC avg margin: +11.0% · Closure: 2.0% · SCH: +4.7% · RCH: 0.0% closure rate
09
Temporal Pattern
COVID Relief Suppressed — But Did Not Resolve — The Structural Crisis
Closures peaked at 17 in 2015 and 2019. The 2021 trough (2 closures) aligned with CARES Act and Provider Relief Fund disbursements. The 2022–2025 rebound (26 closures combined) confirms relief funding masked structural insolvency without addressing its drivers. One-time capital injections cannot substitute for sustainable payment reform.
2019: 17 closures → 2021: 2 closures (CARES) → 2022–25: 26 closures combined
10
Structural Mismatch
Rural Hospitals Are Operationally Subsidy-Dependent — A Structural Risk
Open hospitals average a patient services margin of +2.6% but a total margin of +6.2% — a 3.6pp gap attributable entirely to non-operating revenue: government grants, investment income, and subsidies. Remove non-patient revenue and a substantial share of open hospitals would immediately join the high-risk cohort. Policy must address this dependency, not just its symptoms.
Total vs. patient margin gap: 3.6pp · Non-operating revenue as viability bridge
Section 05
Federal Program Effectiveness Assessment
Dataset-evidenced evaluation of current U.S. rural health policy instruments
Critical Access Hospital Program
CAH · Medicare Flex Program · Cost-Based Reimbursement
78%
Provides 101% of allowable Medicare costs to rural hospitals with ≤25 acute beds located 35+ miles from the nearest hospital. Dataset evidence is strong: 4.9% closure rate — lowest of any major designation. The program directly addresses the volume-margin trap of rural acute care. However, 28.8% of open CAHs remain high-risk, indicating payment protection is necessary but insufficient without operational support.
Effectiveness
78 / 100
Medicare-Dependent Hospital Program
MDH · Expired Post-2022 · Not Reformed
8%
Designed for hospitals with 60%+ Medicare inpatient volume, MDH provided enhanced payment rates as an alternative to CAH conversion. Dataset evidence is damning: 17 of 18 MDH hospitals closed (94.4%). MDH status was repeatedly extended via short-term legislation rather than reformed. The program's architecture — enhanced fee-for-service without structural redesign — failed to prevent the insolvency it was created to avert.
Effectiveness
8 / 100
Rural Emergency Hospital Conversion
REH · Established January 2023 · 45 Conversions
35%
Allows hospitals to convert to emergency-only care (no inpatient beds) in exchange for a 5% Medicare add-on and monthly facility payments (~$272K/month). The REH model is too new for longitudinal closure analysis, but a critical early warning has emerged: 81.8% of open REH hospitals meet the high-risk financial threshold. Whether converting hospitals are deeply distressed at entry or the payment model is insufficient requires immediate CMS investigation.
Effectiveness
35 / 100 (Preliminary)
Medicaid Expansion (ACA)
State Opt-In · 41 States + DC as of 2024
85%
Not exclusively a rural hospital program, but its rural effect is among the most powerful findings in this dataset. Expansion states show a 5.8% rural closure rate versus 11.6% in non-expansion states — a 2× differential. Consistent with national literature showing expansion dramatically reduces uncompensated care burden. Holdout states (TX, FL, TN, AL, KS, OK) account for 101 closures — 52% of the national total.
Effectiveness
85 / 100
HRSA Rural Health Programs
FORHP · Flex Program · Rural Grants
52%
HRSA's Federal Office of Rural Health Policy administers operational grants to CAH hospitals for quality improvement, network integration, and efficiency. Flex grants are modest (~$50K/hospital/year) and cannot offset structural financial losses. CAH performance suggests program contribution is real but marginal; it cannot overcome the fundamental volume-margin mismatch without accompanying payment reform at scale.
Effectiveness
52 / 100
COVID-19 Provider Relief Fund
CARES Act · $178B Total · 2020–2022
42%
Emergency funding temporarily halted rural closures: from 17 (2019) to 2 (2021). However, the 2022–2025 rebound (26 combined closures) confirms this was suppression, not resolution. One-time capital injections masked structural insolvency without addressing its drivers. The program validated that funding can pause closures — but also revealed that sustainable payment architecture, not emergency relief, is the only durable solution.
Effectiveness
42 / 100 (Temporary)
Section 06
Targeted Policy Recommendations
Seven evidence-grounded interventions at the federal and state level, ranked by estimated impact
Federal Level · Payment Reform & Regulatory Action
F1
Establish a Permanent Rural Hospital Viability Fund with Tiered Cost-Based Payments
Replace the patchwork of expiring designations (MDH, SCH) with a unified Rural Viability Fund extending cost-based Medicare reimbursement — scaled by closure risk score — to all rural hospitals under 100 beds with a payer mix exceeding 60% Medicare/Medicaid. Evidence base: MDH's 94.4% closure rate under enhanced fee-for-service confirms that payment uplift without structural architecture fails. Model: CAH's 4.9% closure rate under cost-plus reimbursement.
F2
Mandate CMS Financial Sustainability Review of All REH Conversions at 24 Months
With 81.8% of open REH hospitals flagged high-risk, CMS must conduct mandatory financial assessments for all REH conversions and adjust the 5% add-on payment rate accordingly. If patient margins remain below −10% for the majority of REH facilities at the 24-month mark, the model requires structural redesign. Publish quarterly REH financial performance data for public monitoring.
F3
Institutionalize a Federal Rural Hospital Early Warning System Using Financial Threshold Triggers
Mandate annual CMS reporting that triggers federal technical assistance and bridge funding when: (a) total margin falls below −5% for two consecutive years, (b) patient margin falls below −10%, or (c) composite risk score exceeds a defined threshold. Intervene before the crisis point. Current policy responds to closures — it should prevent them. Funding: redirect a portion of emergency relief reserves to predictive intervention.
State Level · Medicaid, Workforce & Sustainability
S1
Medicaid Expansion Remains the Single Highest-Leverage State Policy Action Available
The dataset provides near-controlled evidence: non-expansion states face 2× the rural closure rate. For Texas, Tennessee, Alabama, Florida, and Kansas — which account for 67 closures combined — Medicaid expansion is the highest-impact, lowest-cost rural hospital intervention available at the state level. Policy model: Kentucky and West Virginia's post-expansion rural hospital stabilization demonstrated in national literature and consistent with this dataset's expansion-state performance.
S2
Create State Rural Hospital Sustainability Funds Targeting the 665 High-Risk Open Facilities
States with high concentrations of at-risk open hospitals — Texas (patient margin −10.2%), Kansas (−9.3%), Oklahoma (−10.1%), Alabama (−8.5%) — should establish state-level sustainability funds providing three-year operational grants to sole-provider rural facilities with total margin below −5%. Prioritize facilities serving majority Medicaid populations and located in counties with no alternative hospital within 20 miles. Condition grants on board-approved financial improvement plans.
S3
Invest in Rural Physician Workforce Pipelines to Address the Volume Shortage Underlying Financial Distress
Margins cannot improve without patient volume; volume requires physician presence. States should: (a) fund Rural Health Scholarships conditioned on 3–5 year rural service commitments; (b) partner with academic medical centers to establish rural-track residency programs; (c) maximize HRSA J-1 visa waiver placements for international medical graduates in HPSA-designated rural areas — IMGs represent 25% of rural physicians nationally and are a systematically underutilized pipeline. Louisiana-specific implication: LSUSOM GME program design should incorporate rural track incentives.
M1
Monitor REH Conversions Quarterly — The Model May Be Failing Before It Has Been Evaluated
REH designation launched in January 2023. The 81.8% high-risk rate among open REH hospitals is a critical early warning that demands immediate policy attention. CMS should publish quarterly REH financial performance data and evaluate whether the $272,866/month facility payment achieves viability. If not, the program risks creating a new category of designated-but-failing facilities — institutionalizing the same failure pattern as MDH under a new label.
Methodology & Data Notes
Analysis based on 2,496 rural hospital records (Source: Saving Rural Hospitals dataset). Closure defined by Closed? field = "Closed." High-risk threshold: total margin <−5% OR patient services margin <−10% (conservative screening criterion). Medicaid expansion status: 41 states + DC = expansion; 10 states = non-expansion (based on 2024 expansion map; NC joined 2023). Statistical tests: independent samples t-test for margin comparisons; results reported as t-statistic and p-value. Composite risk scoring: normalized composite of closure rate (60% weight) and inverse margin (40% weight), scaled 0–100. MDH analysis: n=18 total (17 closed, 1 open). Policy effectiveness ratings are qualitative-quantitative hybrid assessments integrating dataset evidence with published literature — not pure model outputs. All dollar figures in USD. Analysis: DataDrivenMed · datadrivenmed.github.io · April 2025.
